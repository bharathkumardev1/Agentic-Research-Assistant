Dense Retrieval with Contrastive Pretraining for Literature Search

Authors: H. Nakamura, S. Adeyemi, T. Varga (synthetic example paper)

Abstract
Effective retrieval is the foundation of any grounded question-answering
system. We present ConText, a dense passage retriever pretrained with a
contrastive objective over 8 million scientific abstracts. ConText improves
top-5 retrieval recall on a literature-search benchmark from 0.62 to 0.79 over
a sparse BM25 baseline, and transfers to unseen subfields with minimal
degradation.

Methods
We mine positive passage pairs from citation links and section co-occurrence,
and sample hard negatives using an in-batch strategy refreshed every epoch.
Passages are encoded into 384-dimensional embeddings that are L2-normalised so
that inner-product search recovers cosine similarity. We index embeddings with
a flat inner-product structure for exact search and evaluate approximate
variants for scale. Retrieval quality is measured with recall@k and mean
reciprocal rank.

Results
ConText raises recall@5 to 0.79 and MRR to 0.61, outperforming BM25 (0.62 /
0.44) and a general-purpose dense encoder (0.71 / 0.52). Hard-negative
refreshing accounts for roughly half of the improvement over the general
encoder. On three held-out subfields, recall drops by only 3 points on average,
indicating reasonable domain transfer. Exact inner-product search remains fast
enough for interactive use at the evaluated corpus size.

Limitations
Contrastive pretraining is computationally expensive and depends on the quality
of mined citation links, which are noisy in emerging fields. ConText was not
evaluated on full-text retrieval, only abstracts, and the approximate index
variants trade a small amount of recall for speed in ways that were not
characterised for very large corpora. No experiment measured calibration of the
similarity scores themselves.
