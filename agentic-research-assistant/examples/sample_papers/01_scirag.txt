Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Scientific Question Answering

Authors: A. Rivera, J. Okafor, M. Lindqvist (synthetic example paper)

Abstract
We study retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) for answering questions over a
corpus of scientific articles. Standard large language models hallucinate
citations and conflate findings across papers. We propose SciRAG, a pipeline
that grounds every generated sentence in retrieved passages and attaches
inline citation markers. On a benchmark of 1,200 expert-written questions,
SciRAG reduces unsupported claims by 63% relative to a non-retrieval baseline
while improving answer correctness.

Methods
SciRAG splits each paper into overlapping passages of roughly 1,000 characters
and embeds them with a contrastively trained dense encoder. At query time we
retrieve the top-k passages by cosine similarity from a FAISS index and prompt
the generator to cite passage identifiers. A lightweight verifier re-checks
each sentence against its cited passage and flags sentences whose claims are
not entailed by the citation. Flagged sentences trigger a second retrieval
round with a query expanded by the verifier.

Results
SciRAG attains 81.4% answer correctness versus 68.0% for the closed-book
baseline and 74.2% for a single-pass RAG system without verification. The
verifier's re-retrieval step contributes a further 4.1 points. Human raters
judged 92% of SciRAG citations to be relevant, compared with 61% for the
single-pass system. Latency increases by 1.7x due to the verification round.

Limitations
The verifier itself can miss subtle unsupported inferences, and the approach
assumes the answer is contained in the corpus. SciRAG was evaluated only on
English-language biomedical and machine-learning papers; generalisation to
other domains and languages is untested. The dense encoder was not stress
tested on out-of-distribution terminology, and no ablation isolates the effect
of chunk size on retrieval quality.
