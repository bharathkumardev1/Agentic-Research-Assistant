Multi-Agent Reflection Improves Long-Form Reasoning

Authors: P. Mensah, L. Conti, R. Bhattacharya (synthetic example paper)

Abstract
We investigate whether decomposing a reasoning task across cooperating agents
improves the quality of long-form answers. Our framework, ReflectGraph, runs a
proposer agent that drafts an answer, a critic agent that evaluates grounding
and completeness, and a controller that decides whether to revise. Across
three reasoning benchmarks, ReflectGraph improves factual accuracy by 11 points
and reduces logical inconsistencies by 38% compared with single-pass
generation.

Methods
ReflectGraph is implemented as a directed graph of agents with a bounded
revision loop. The proposer produces a structured answer with explicit claims.
The critic scores each claim for support and coverage and, when coverage is
low, emits a refined sub-question. The controller terminates when the critic
reports sufficiency or a maximum of three revision cycles is reached. All
agents share a common evidence pool so revisions accumulate context rather than
discarding it.

Results
On scientific synthesis tasks, ReflectGraph reaches 0.86 coverage as judged by
experts, versus 0.71 for a single proposer. The bounded loop converges in 1.8
revisions on average. Disabling the critic removes most of the gain, confirming
that explicit evaluation, not merely additional generation, drives the
improvement. The controller's stopping rule avoids the degenerate behaviour of
endlessly rewriting confident-but-wrong answers.

Limitations
The critic can be over-confident and occasionally halts before coverage is
truly adequate. Costs scale with the number of revision cycles, and the method
assumes the proposer and critic do not share the same systematic blind spots.
Experiments did not measure robustness to adversarial or contradictory evidence
in the shared pool, and human evaluation was limited to a single annotator pool.
